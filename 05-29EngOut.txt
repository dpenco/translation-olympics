35. since the late 1980s, the work of the Committee has focused on reviewing other income concepts.

The basic concept of this adjustment is that the practice of reducing debt from national income is not an income concept defined in international standards.
37. monetary income is defined as national income MINUS the estimated income from subsistence production in agriculture and other activities.
The income calculated according to this concept will benefit low-income countries with large self-sufficient economies.
However, the concept is not very sound, so it is not included in the international statistical standards and cannot be expressed quantitatively.
This concept will better reflect the accrued assessment income of countries with large numbers of migrant workers or migrant workers employed abroad and countries with large numbers of refugees.
39. income adjusted for changes in national wealth is defined as national income less the interest expense required to make investments to replace the loss of capital assets destroyed or lost by natural or man-made disasters.
The formulation of this concept is not very sound and has not been put into practice. Therefore, they are not included in the international statistical standards and statistics are not easily available.
40. sustainable income is defined as national income MINUS future expenditures needed to maintain this income.
The concept has not been effectively formulated. it is not included in the international statistical standards and the provision of statistics depends on how the concept is formulated.
If further elaboration is made, the concept will face up to the needs expressed by some member States, that is, adjusting their national income to compensate for a country's shortcomings in social and economic development ( for example, lack of health care or educational facilities, housing and transportation systems ).
41. the discussion of the alternative income concept showed that the members of the Committee had many different opinions.

However, members believe that the alternative income concept discussed above should continue to be considered and its possible use reviewed regularly.
44. the market exchange rate is used for international trade in goods and services and other payments and receipts between countries.
They reflect the relative prices of goods and services between countries and other international transactions ( such as foreign investment and loans, income and expenditure from investment and remittances of workers, working capital transfer ), as well as interest rates and expectations of financial markets.
The advantage of using market exchange rates to calculate the scale of assessments is that this exchange rate is the same as the exchange rate used to calculate the payment of assessed contributions.
These data are available to all countries except IMF members.
All market exchange rates published by IMF are in line with international comparability standards.
45. the price adjustment exchange rate was established by the statistics division because the national income of countries with fixed and controlled exchange rates and / or rates in the case of high inflation were not normal in the us dollar equivalent.
The definition of price adjustment exchange rate is to adjust the base period exchange rate of previous years or later years by price index that reflects inflation.
The market exchange rate and the price adjustment exchange rate for the base period are equal.
Price adjustment of exchange rate has eliminated the influence of high inflation rate and unstable changes of exchange rate for many years.
These exchange rates have stabilized the time series of per capita income over the years.
As long as the price-adjusted exchange rate is based on the current exchange rate for the base period, illustrative calculations show that their application has not greatly changed the distribution of national income among member States.
46. apart from the still existing practical problems about the base period used, the formulation of the concept of price-adjusted exchange rate is very good for the purpose of preparing the scale of assessments.
One of the benefits of using price-adjusted exchange rates is to reduce fluctuations in national income and per capita income over a period of time and to have the opportunity to use a shorter statistical base period.
However, it was pointed out that price-adjusted exchange rates could only be useful for calculating assessments, but not for actual contributions.
47. a country's purchasing power parity is defined as how many units of a country's currency are needed to buy the same amount of goods and services in its own market, i.e. the same amount of goods and services as can be bought in a dollar in the United States.
In other words, purchasing power parity focuses on what kind of goods and services a country's GDP will buy compared with the purchasing power of other countries' GDP, instead of using international exchange rates to evaluate the output of goods and services in us dollars.
For the preparation of the scale, the use of purchasing power parity based on market exchange rates may greatly change the current distribution of national income.
48. the concept of purchasing power parity is very sound. several international and regional organizations use it for analysis and some regional organizations use it for policy formulation.
At the same time, purchasing power parity is more suitable for international products than income used in preparing the scale.
This concept is based on the assumption that the goods and services selected for comparison are actually comparable in different countries.
Necessary statistics are collected every five years for a few countries.
49. the exchange rate of the world atlas is the average of the market exchange rate and the two price-adjusted exchange rates in the previous year, and the exchange rates in the previous two years are revised to this year with the price index.
The concept of world atlas exchange rate is sound and is more suitable for international comparison than purchasing power parity.
Currently the world bank ( world atlas ) and UNDP ( indicative planning figures ) are using it, although not included in international statistical standards; However, statistics are available every year for countries included in the world atlas.
50. in discussing the issue of different conversion rates, the members of the Committee attempted to accommodate their limitations on different concepts and data on the one hand and the extent to which they provided accurate and stable income measures over a period of time on the other.
General members agree that the problem of interpreting data, the problem of providing data and the cost of data compilation at this time are more important than the interesting characteristics of purchasing power parity ( for example, making the distribution of national income more stable will shorten the statistical base period ).
At the same time, the issue of concern was whether the concept of international income comparison was appropriate and the chaotic impact on the distribution of national income caused by the initial use of the concept for the preparation of the scale.
Those members who are not in favour of using PPPs in the preparation of the scale emphasize that PPPs are currently only used for analysis and research and for the formulation of limited policies.
51. since there is no significant difference between the world atlas exchange rate and the market exchange rate after a period of time and because the latter is relatively popular, the question of the most appropriate conversion rate has become a choice between the market exchange rate and the price-adjusted exchange rate.
At the same time, as in the past, price-adjusted exchange rates can also be used to correct exchange rate distortions in several countries.
52. the Committee pointed out the reasons for changing the statistical base period and considered the issue at various times in the past.
The period of the statistical base period used in the current scale methodology is the best guarantee for the stability of the scale, which balances the increase and decrease in national income.
However, the period of the current base period represents the past rather than current capacity to pay of member States.
54. adjusting national income based on per capita income has enabled many developing countries, especially those with a very large population, to reduce their burden.

44. The market exchange rate is used for the transactions of international goods and services and other payments and receipts between countries.
They reflect the relative prices of goods and services between countries and other international transactions (such as foreign investment and loans, income and expenditure of investment income, and workers' remittances, current capital transfers), as well as interest rates and expectations for financial markets.
The advantage of using market exchange rates to calculate the scale of assessments is that the exchange rate is the same as the exchange rate used to calculate the payment of assessed contributions.
Unless IMF members, these data are available for all countries.
All market exchange rates announced by the IMF are in line with international standards of comparability.
45. The price-adjusted exchange rate is set by the Statistics Division because the dollar equivalent of the national income of countries in which they are: fixed and regulated exchange rates and/or rates in the case of high inflation rates.
The price-adjusted exchange rate is defined as the reference period exchange rate for the years or years following the adjustment of the price index reflecting inflation.
The benchmark market exchange rate and the price adjustment exchange rate are equal.
The price-adjusted exchange rate has for many years eliminated the effects of high inflation rates and volatile changes in exchange rates.
These exchange rates have stabilized the per capita income time series over the years.
As long as the price-adjusted exchange rate is based on the current exchange rate for the reference period, the illustrative calculations show that their application did not significantly change the distribution of national income among Member States.

However, it was pointed out that the price-adjusted exchange rate can only be useful for calculating the amount of assessment, but it is useless for the actual payment.
47. A countrys purchasing power parity is defined as how many units of a countrys currency are required to buy the same quantity of goods and services in the domestic market, ie, goods and services that can be purchased for a dollar in the United States.
In other words, the purchasing power parity is a measure of what kind of goods and services a country's gross domestic product will buy compared to the purchasing power of other countries' gross domestic product, rather than using international exchange rates to evaluate the output of goods and services that countries use in dollars. .
In order to compile the scale, the use of purchasing power parity based on market exchange rates may greatly change the current distribution of national income.
48. The concept of purchasing power parity is very robust and it is used by several international and regional organizations for analysis. Some regional organizations also use it to formulate policies.
At the same time, purchasing power parity is also more suitable for international products than, for example, the income used in the preparation of scales.
The concept is based on the assumption that goods and services selected for comparison are actually comparable in different countries.
Every five years, the necessary statistics are collected for a few countries.
49. The world atlas exchange rate is the average of the market exchange rate of the previous year and the two price-adjusted exchange rates. The exchange rates of these exchange rates in the past two years have been revised to this year using the price index.
The concept of the world atlas exchange rate is sound and is also more suitable for international comparison than purchasing power parity.
The World Bank (World Atlas) and UNDP (indicative planning figures) are currently using it, although not included in international statistical standards; however, there are statistics for countries included in the World Atlas every year.
